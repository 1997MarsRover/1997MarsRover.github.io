Graph Neural Networks

Grounded in the structure of deep learning, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are a type of neural network designed to work directly with graph-structured data. This is in contrast to traditional neural networks that rely on grid-structured data.

As Michael Bronstein notes in his book "Geometric Deep Learning: Grids, Graphs, and Geodesics", GNNs can be viewed as a generalization of traditional neural networks to graph-structured data.

Mathematically, a GNN can be represented as a composition of the following layers:

1. Graph convolutional layer: This layer applies a convolutional operation to the graph-structured data, where the convolutional operation is defined as:

H = σ(U * X * W)

where H is the output of the layer, U is the adjacency matrix of the graph, X is the input data, W is the weight matrix, and σ is the activation function.

2. Graph pooling layer: This layer reduces the spatial dimensions of the graph-structured data, typically using a pooling operation such as max-pooling or average-pooling.

3. Graph readout layer: This layer generates a fixed-size vector representation of the graph-structured data, typically using a permutation-invariant function such as sum-pooling or mean-pooling.
